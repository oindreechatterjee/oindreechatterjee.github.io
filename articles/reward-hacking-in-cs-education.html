Reward Hacking in CS Education

March 8, 2026 · 5 min read · (originally published on Medium)

Reward hacking is what happens when an AI system follows the letter of the law but ignores the spirit. It’s an RL agent finding a glitch to get infinite points instead of actually playing the game; it’s optimizing for a proxy metric while the real goal(s) get left behind. In recent research, Anthropic has shown this happens even in advanced coding models, where an AI might find a loophole to pass a test suite without actually solving the logic.

I’ve been sitting with this concept lately, specifically how it applies to the CS education space. A talk I attended recently by a Google UX researcher finally made the connection click: in the age of GenAI, the “reward” is the grade, and we are all at risk of hacking it (and nobody would call that a win!).

The talk was called “Google’s Approach to AI Tools for Software Engineers,” given by Dr. Jamie Benario at SIGCSE TS 2026. Benario walked us through how Google thinks about deploying AI tools internally—both for coding tasks (code assistance, bug fixing, migration, testing) and non-coding ones (email writing, document drafting, performance reviews). She also talked about how Google actually trains its software engineers to use these tools: through workshops, orientation, empowering team leads, and building always-available resources.

What struck me wasn’t the tool list, but rather the measurement infrastructure underneath it all.

Benario described a framework: 75 tasks that align with 12 work-related goals of developers working at the cutting edge of AI-powered tooling. There’s also ongoing research into understanding the sentiment of how Googlers actually feel about AI adoption. That’s a lot of scaffolding dedicated to understanding whether AI is working. But working how, exactly? Measured how?

If you’re Google, your metrics that define “successful AI adoption” may include elements like task completion speed, code quality scores, reduction in bug reports, or even survey responses about satisfaction.

These may be reasonable to measure in a corporate context. For instance, if a developer ships better code faster because they leaned heavily on Gemini Code Assist, the outcome is genuinely good. Reward hacking isn’t really a concern when the proxy and the goal are well-aligned.

But in the education space, the proxy and the goal can come apart fast.

Benario touched on this directly, citing a UC Berkeley study and referencing findings from CS education researchers: generative AI tends to be more useful for students who are already experienced, and could be actively harmful for novice programmers. The paper she mentioned—“The Widening Gap: Benefits and Harms of Generative AI for Novice Programmers”—captures this tension perfectly. A novice student using AI to complete assignments might produce output that looks correct. Their grade (the benchmark) goes up. But their actual understanding? That’s harder to measure, and it may not be going up at all.

That’s reward hacking. Not by the AI, but by the student, often unintentionally. They’re optimizing for the grade, the visible metric, while the thing we actually care about (building foundational understanding) gets left out.

At this point, Benario made a statement that further got me thinking.

“Feelings of over-reliance may actually positively influence student metacognition.”

Most people assume over-reliance on AI is straightforwardly bad. But the Berkeley study suggests something more interesting: when students feel like they’re leaning on AI too much, that uncomfortable feeling actually makes them think more carefully about their own learning. Am I actually understanding this, or am I just regurgitating what the AI says? The discomfort becomes productive.

This creates a weird problem for anyone trying to measure whether AI is working in education. If you’re a teacher or researcher, the metrics you’d naturally reach for are whether the student finished the assignment, how much they used the AI, or whether their output was correct. But none of those capture what actually matters—whether the student is genuinely learning and developing as a thinker.

And if the feeling of over-reliance is what’s triggering that self-reflection, then “reliance” is actually a terrible thing to optimize against. You could design a whole intervention to reduce AI dependence, measure success by lower usage rates, and accidentally eliminate the exact discomfort that was making students more self-aware of their learning.

That’s the reward hacking problem in full force. We hit the metric, but we lost the point.

Benario ended with two lasting questions I want to leave here, because I don’t think they have clean answers:

  1. Is reliance always a bad thing? Is student perception of reliance a valuable measure when evaluating the success of a GenAI intervention?
  2. Both industry and education suggest that experience is required to be a good user of generative AI. So how do we support learners in building that experience, while those tools are already available to them?

The second question hits hardest for me personally. As a UC Berkeley EECS student who also does research in the CS education space, I’m living in this tension. I use AI tools. My peers use AI tools. The students I think about in my research use AI tools. We’re all already past the point of “should we introduce this?” The question is how you build real competence in an environment where the shortcut is always one tab away. Personally, it comes down to the student’s thirst for learning. But for those who need more structure, redesigning how we measure success may help: oral exams, handwritten work, or assignments that build on AI output rather than just produce it. Exploring AI-driven metacognitive tools could be insightful as well (ways to use AI to actually force that productive discomfort rather than just going back to pen and paper methods).

If we’re limiting measuring success to task completion and grades, we may never notice when we’ve “reward hacked” our way to the wrong outcome.
